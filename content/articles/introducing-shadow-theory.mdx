Shadow Theory is the public framework of this site. Its foundation is a
seven-paper canonical sequence, read in order: six papers of source–readout
mathematics, and a seventh that realizes the whole architecture in a concrete
physical model.

## The idea in one paragraph

Bounded descriptions — measurements, macrostates, summaries, public
presentations — are *readouts* of richer structure. Paper 1 proves that an
exact readout is not the same thing as the source it summarizes: after
declared redundancy is quotiented out, the readout presents the source exactly
as a quotient, yet a physically invariant relation can fail to be a function
of readout data, and no symmetry-compatible rule need recover the realized
state. The remaining papers develop what follows with unusual discipline:
whether lost structure matters is a property of the *question asked*
(Paper 2); the coarsest completion on which nominated relations become well
defined is canonical — terminal, unique, selector-free (Paper 3); such
completions are realized by genuine geometry only on a precisely bounded
domain (Paper 4); observable dynamics close autonomously under an exact
kernel-preservation condition, and otherwise carry exact memory (Paper 5);
and a model with an essential non-gauge fiber distinction is, relative to
that model and target, a *non-source projection*, with hard ceilings on what
internal observers can identify (Paper 6). Paper 7 then supplies the physical
witness: in Randall–Sundrum (RS2) gravity, gauge-inequivalent bulk states
with identical brane readout evolve to different brane futures.

<ClaimBoundary>
Shadow Theory is a public mathematical framework: definitions, theorems, and
claim discipline. Every result is model-relative — the papers do not claim
that our universe has been proved to be a projection of a hidden source, and
Paper 7's witness is a theorem about the RS2 model, not an unrestricted
ontological claim. The framework is not empirically validated physics and is
not peer reviewed unless a specific record states otherwise.
</ClaimBoundary>

## Why the discipline matters

Most frameworks fail publicly in one of two ways: they claim too much, or they
leave the boundary between "proved" and "hoped" implicit. Shadow Theory makes
that boundary part of the mathematics. Each paper states exactly what its
hypotheses are, what its theorems establish, and what they do *not* establish
— Paper 6 is explicitly a boundary theorem, and Paper 7 proves its own
interpretive limit: no brane-only experiment can distinguish the
five-dimensional source model from its exact four-dimensional pushforward.
